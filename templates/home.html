The Research

A global food crisis is quietly taking shape

Two landmark studies published in Nature Communications and PLOS ONE by Ray et al. (2012, 2013) analysed ~2.5 million agricultural census observations across 13,500 political units globally, tracking maize, rice, wheat and soybean from 1961 to 2008. Their findings are sobering.

"Across 24–39% of maize-, rice-, wheat- and soybean-growing areas, yields either never improve, stagnate or collapse."

Ray et al. — Nature Communications, 2012 (DOI: 10.1038/ncomms2296)

The four crops covered by this research - maize, rice, wheat and soybean — produce nearly two-thirds of global agricultural calories. When yields stagnate across more than a third of their growing areas, the implications for food security are profound.

Maize 26% of harvested area experiencing yield stagnation globally
Rice 35% of harvested area stagnating — including 79% of China's rice land
Wheat 37% of harvested area stagnating — almost all of Western Europe
Soybean 23% of harvested area not witnessing yield improvement

The causes are location-specific but include climate-related heat stress, soil fertility depletion, salinisation, groundwater depletion, increasing pest resistance, and — particularly in Africa — a lack of irrigation infrastructure and agricultural investment. The common thread is that the problem is knowable and, in many cases, addressable with the right information.

Updated 2025 New Research

It's no longer just stagnation — climate is now actively pulling yields backwards

A landmark 2025 study in Nature (Hultgren et al.) measured real-world farmer adaptation across 12,000 regions and found climate change will reduce global crop yields by 8% by 2050 regardless of emissions trajectory — with losses reaching 24% by 2100 if emissions continue unchecked. Even accounting for everything farmers can do, adaptation only offsets about one-third of these losses.

The 2050 Gap

Current growth rates are not enough

Ray et al. (2013) calculated that global crop production must roughly double by 2050 to meet demand — requiring a sustained 2.4% per year yield improvement rate. None of the four key crops are achieving this. At current rates, production would increase by far less than required, leaving a staggering shortfall.

Maize
1.6%/yr needs 2.4%
At current rates, production increases only ~67% by 2050 — a shortfall of 247 million tonnes versus what demand requires.
Rice
1.0%/yr needs 2.4%
Only ~42% increase projected by 2050. China, India and Indonesia — the top three producers — are all growing at well below doubling rates.
Wheat
0.9%/yr needs 2.4%
The weakest performer. Only ~38% production growth projected — a 388 million tonne shortfall by 2050. Western Europe has nearly universally stagnating wheat yields.
Soybean
1.3%/yr needs 2.4%
~55% increase projected — better than wheat and rice but still insufficient. South America is the bright spot, with doubling rates in much of Argentina and Brazil.
The Solution

What Crop Companion does

Armed with this evidence, we built a tool designed to give farmers and agricultural researchers the predictive intelligence they need — trained on real FAO census data, not synthetic datasets.

01
Yield Prediction

An ensemble of Random Forest, XGBoost and LightGBM models trained on real FAOSTAT data across 100+ countries and 40+ crops. Input your area, crop, rainfall, temperature and pesticide use — get a calibrated yield forecast with model-level breakdown.

02
Sustainability Advisory

Every prediction includes a full sustainability report: water requirements vs. rainfall, irrigation gap, carbon footprint from pesticides, and actionable recommendations calibrated to your income level — including low-cost alternatives for smallholder farmers.

03 🔬
Disease Detection

Upload a photo of a diseased leaf and our ResNet-18 deep learning model - trained on real field photographs — identifies the disease, assesses severity, and prescribes organic, chemical and preventative treatment protocols tailored to the specific pathogen.

04
Pest Identification

Photograph an insect or pest damage and our ResNet-18 model identifies it from 15 dangerous farm pest classes — including aphids, armyworms, corn borers, spider mites and more — then delivers targeted management, chemical control, and prevention guidance.

05
CropGPT: Ask an Expert

No photo? Just ask. Our AI-powered agricultural advisor answers questions about diseases, pest management, irrigation, soil health and sustainable farming, with practical, farmer-friendly advice for both organic and conventional operations.
